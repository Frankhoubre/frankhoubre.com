Producing a clean AI video is already difficult. Delivering it professionally is another level. Many creators lose time, margin, and sometimes the client, not on the quality of the clip, but on the absence of clear reporting.

The subject **client PDF reporting: professional AI video deliverables** is not glamorous, but it is what turns a "creative freelancer" into a reliable partner. A good delivery PDF reduces fuzzy feedback, legally protects both parties, and demonstrates a process mastery that reassures decision-makers.

In this guide, we structure a Frank template: synthetic, actionable, and ready to industrialize for an agency, a studio or a premium solo.

## Why reporting becomes critical in AI video

With AI video, the retouches can seem infinite. With no delivery frame:

- the client does not know what is "finished" vs "perfectible",
- the team forgets the decisions taken,
- the contractual risks increase,
- the deadlines slip.

A well-built PDF serves as project memory and operational contract.

> 💡 **Frank's Cut:** the client forgives an owned micro visual defect. They do not forgive a fuzziness about what was delivered, validated, and accepted.

## The 8 sections of a pro delivery PDF

### 1) Executive summary

Half a page:

- project goal,
- delivered deliverables,
- global status (validated / validated with reservations).

### 2) Production scope

Clearly describe:

- what is AI-generated,
- what is shot for real,
- what is retouched in post.

### 3) File versioning

List:

- master names,
- formats,
- resolutions,
- durations,
- generation date.

### 4) Quality and QA criteria

Recall the criteria used:

- visual consistency,
- mobile readability,
- shot stability,
- audio intelligibility.

### 5) Owned visual debts

Very important:

- what was not corrected,
- why,
- expected impact.

### 6) Legal mentions and rights

Include:

- asset usage frame,
- known limits,
- credit obligations if applicable.

### 7) Distribution recommendations

Per platform:

- format,
- codec,
- loudness,
- subtitles yes/no.

### 8) Maintenance plan

What is planned post-delivery:

- correction window,
- retouch conditions,
- support delays.

## Reporting workflow in production

### Phase 0: collect along the way

Do not wait for the end to document. During the prod:

- note the art-direction decisions,
- archive the validated versions,
- capture the key settings.

You build your PDF progressively.

### Phase 1: lock the technical delivery

Before writing:

- list the final exports,
- check checksums if necessary,
- validate that everything matches what is announced to the client.

### Phase 2: write the PDF

Your tone must be:

- factual,
- transparent,
- non-defensive.

Example of a useful formulation:

`Shot 03 presents a slight texture variation on the right sleeve at 00:04. This variation was judged acceptable in view of the mobile distribution format.`

### Phase 3: cross-check

Have it reread by someone not involved in the edit. If they understand the delivery without asking you ten questions, your PDF is good.

### Phase 4: sending and archiving

Send:

- PDF,
- deliverables folder,
- a short accompanying note.

Archive everything in a stable structure.

## How to document the visual debts without shooting yourself in the foot

The word "debt" is scary. Yet, well formulated, it reassures.

Recommended format:

- **Observation**: factual description.
- **Impact**: low / medium / high.
- **Context**: why not corrected.
- **Option**: possible correction under quote if necessary.

Example:

`Observation: micro instability of the background reflection shot 05. Impact: low in mobile distribution. Context: the correction risked altering the product readability. Option: local treatment possible in an advanced retouch batch.`

You show mastery, not weakness.

## Common reporting mistakes

**Mistake 1: too-marketing PDF.**  
Solution: favor the facts, not the self-promotion.

**Mistake 2: lack of versioning.**  
Solution: strict nomenclature.

**Mistake 3: no mention of the AI limits.**  
Solution: proactive transparency.

**Mistake 4: delivered files not aligned with the PDF.**  
Solution: final cross-check.

**Mistake 5: no distribution recommendation.**

**Mistake 6: fuzzy legal language.**  
Solution: simple sentences, validated with the contractual frame.

## FAQ

**How many pages should a client PDF reporting be?**

Between 6 and 12 pages according to complexity. Shorter, you risk forgetting. Longer, no one reads.

**Should you mention all the AI limits?**

Yes, the ones that can affect use, perception or responsibility. Targeted transparency, not an anxious novel.

**Who validates the PDF before sending?**

Ideally a duo: production + account direction. Technical and relational must be aligned.

**Should I include the prompts in the PDF?**

Not necessarily in detail. You can append a methodological summary and keep the complete prompts in internal archive, except contractual request.

**How to handle out-of-scope retouch requests?**

The PDF must plan a "post-delivery options" section with a frame of time and cost.

**Can the client refuse an announced visual debt?**

Yes. The interest of documenting it is precisely to clarify this point before a dispute.

**What is the difference between deliverable and archive?**

The deliverable serves the distribution. The archive serves the reproducibility and the future maintenance.

**Should you attach the separate audio sources?**

According to the contract. Generally, attach at least a stereo master and the elements necessary for the planned adaptations.

**How to secure the understanding on the non-technical client side?**

Add a short glossary and simple formulations. Avoid the gratuitous jargon.

**What is the number-one signal of pro reporting?**

The consistency: what is announced in the PDF corresponds exactly to the files received.

A successful client reporting does not only serve to "look clean". It protects the project, speeds up the validations, and gives credibility to your studio for the future. In AI, this discipline makes the difference between talented tinkering and solid production.

**Typical session (90 min)**: 20 min collection, 20 min structuring, 25 min writing, 15 min cross-check, 10 min export/sending.

**Final checklist**: clear scope, exact deliverables table, owned debts, legal mentions present, distribution recommendations included, organized archive.

## Annexes that save time

The main PDF must stay readable. The heavy details go in annexes. It is what lets you be complete without drowning your client.

Useful annexes:

- version journal;
- QA captures (annotated images);
- table of codecs and export parameters;
- subtitles and accessibility sheet;
- proof of authorizations and licenses.

You can attach the annexes in a second PDF or in a separate folder. The important thing: they exist and are referenced in the main report.

## How to write the "legal mentions" with no indigestible jargon

Simple rule:

- short sentence;
- common vocabulary;
- explicit scope.

Example:

`Some elements of the project were generated or assisted by AI. The authorized uses correspond to the scope defined in the contract. Any extension of use (new territory, new medium, additional duration) requires prior validation.`

The goal is not to impress. The goal is to be clear.

- One debts table.

- PDF 8 to 12 pages.

Adapt the level of detail to the risk and the budget.

## Client validation: anti-fuzzy-feedback protocol

Add a "validation terms" section:

- feedback delay (e.g. 5 business days),
- expected feedback format (timecoded comments),
- status in case of no feedback.

This protocol turns "we do not like it" into actionable feedback.

## Mini editorial charter for a client PDF

Your PDF must be:

- factual;
- non-defensive;
- solution-oriented;
- visually consistent.

Avoid:

- sarcasm;
- excessive self-justification;
- unexplained jargon.

The "calm pro" tone is the one that defuses tensions.

## Crisis case: when a client contests after validation

If it happens:

1. Refer to the PDF and the dated validations.
2. Reformulate the contested point in measurable terms.
3. Propose correction options with a delay/cost impact.

Do not enter an abstract debate of taste. Come back to the accepted frame.

## Advanced FAQ

**Does the client really read the PDF?**

Not always in full. But the key sections are consulted during the decisions and the tensions. It is precisely there that it must be impeccable.

**Should I show all the imperfections?**

Show the ones that impact use, perceived quality or responsibility. The goal is useful transparency, not excessive self-criticism.

**Can you automate part of the reporting?**

Yes: deliverables table, metadata, versioning. But the qualitative analysis and the owned debts must stay human.

**Must the PDF be beautiful or just clear?**

Both, but priority to clarity. A clean layout reinforces the trust.

**Should you include the costs in the delivery PDF?**

Not necessarily. Often better in the commercial proposal or the addendum. The delivery PDF stays technical and decisional.

**How to handle a client who asks for "one more small retouch"?**

Refer to the planned retouch scope. Propose a simple and fast mini-addendum.

**Should I deliver the source files?**

Only if planned. Clarify this point in the contract + reporting.

**What is the difference between "validated version" and "delivered version"?**

Validated = accepted on preview. Delivered = final package exported + documented.

**How long to keep the archives?**

According to the contract and the legal constraints, but a minimum sufficient to cover the exploitation period and the possible disputes.

**Is the reporting useful for small clients?**

Yes. Even a lightened version improves the relationship and reduces the misunderstandings.

The client reporting is not an administrative extra. It is part of the product. The clearer it is, the more your creative work is understood, protected and valued.

## "Pre-send to client" checklist in 5 minutes

Before clicking send:

- open the PDF on mobile to check the readability;
- check that the file names match exactly the deliverables;
- control the date and the version in the header;
- reread the visual debts section to avoid any fuzziness;
- confirm that the annex links work.

This final control avoids absurd back-and-forths.

And above all: always send a short accompanying message with three key points (what is delivered, what is validated, what stays optional). The PDF gives the depth. The message gives the direction.

When you combine a readable reporting, a clean nomenclature and an explicit validation frame, you mechanically reduce the project friction. This trio is worth more than a long improvised email sent at midnight.

If you want a fast quality test: give your PDF to a person external to the project and ask them to explain in two minutes what was delivered, validated and potentially to be redone. If they manage, your reporting is good. If they hesitate, you must simplify the structure or clarify the vocabulary.

Clarity stays the best validation accelerator.

And it is also an excellent marker of perceived professionalism.
